Arne Vodder's OS36: Sibast's Rarest Teak Sideboard

By Marco van den Bergh · July 20, 2026

Some sideboards ask to be noticed. The Arne Vodder OS36 asks to be discovered. Close the tambour doors and it reads as a quiet, floating rectangle of teak — open them, and a fully finished interior reveals itself, bar compartment included. That contrast, restraint on the outside, generosity within, is the clearest signature of Vodder's work for Sibast Møbelfabrik.

Vodder designed the OS36 in Denmark in the early 1960s, at the height of Sibast's reputation for cabinetry that treated storage as architecture rather than afterthought. The tambour doors, thin slats of teak running on a track, slide back into the case rather than swinging open, so the piece can sit anywhere in a room without needing clearance. Behind the right-hand door sits a mirrored bar nook, a small theatrical flourish typical of Danish cabinetmaking at its most confident. The back panel is fully finished too, meaning the sideboard was built to float in the middle of a room, not hide against a wall.

What makes an OS36 genuinely rare is the combination: tambour doors, bar compartment and finished back rarely all survive in one example, and Sibast produced fewer case pieces than seating, so surviving stock is thin. Most on the market today show up in teak, occasionally rosewood, both prized for how the grain deepens with age rather than fades.

Reading the teak is the fastest way to judge a piece. Authentic 1960s teak carries a warm, honey-to-amber tone with visible grain movement and a handful of honest surface marks, sun-fading on the top, the odd ring from a glass decades ago. That patina is not damage; it is the piece proving its age. Watch the joinery on the tambour track and the drawer runners: Sibast's construction is tight and solid even sixty years on, where later reproductions tend to feel thin and light in the hand.

Pieces like this move quickly once collectors recognize the model number, which is exactly why Vintz photographs and documents every OS36-type sideboard that passes through, teak grain, hardware, joinery, so buyers know precisely what they are looking at before it ships. Pair one with a set of Danish teak dining chairs from the same era for a room that reads as one considered decade rather than a mismatch of eras.
