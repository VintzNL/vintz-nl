When the Memphis Group erupted onto the design scene in Milan in 1981, its influence rippled far beyond furniture and into every corner of Italian craft — including the centuries-old glass workshops of Murano. This table lamp by Vetri Murano captures that electric moment when traditional Venetian glassblowing met the rebellious energy of postmodern design.

The lamp is hand-blown Murano glass, produced in the 1980s when the Memphis aesthetic was at its peak. The form is playful and graphic, trading the organic curves typical of earlier Murano production for bolder, more geometric gestures. The color work reflects the Memphis palette: confident, layered, and deliberately dissonant in the way that Ettore Sottsass and his circle championed.

This particular piece is in original condition and represents an interesting footnote in Italian design history — the collision between one of the world's oldest decorative arts traditions and one of the twentieth century's most radical design movements. Vetri Murano was among several workshops that adapted their repertoire to meet the new demand for expressive, colorful objects that could hold their own in a Memphis-influenced interior.

The Memphis Group, founded by Sottsass in 1981, included designers like Michele De Lucchi, Nathalie Du Pasquier, and Matteo Thun. Their work was polarizing — Karl Lagerfeld famously furnished his Monaco apartment entirely in Memphis, while others dismissed it as visual noise. Today, Memphis pieces are among the most sought-after collectibles in twentieth-century design, and adjacent pieces like this Vetri Murano lamp ride that same wave of renewed appreciation.

As a table lamp, it does what the best Memphis-adjacent objects do: it makes you smile, it starts a conversation, and it refuses to blend quietly into the background. A small but potent piece for anyone who appreciates design with personality.
