Italian design in the 1970s had a particular talent for turning everyday furniture into something theatrical, and this dining set is a perfect example. A round table and four matching chairs, finished in a deep, dark red lacquer that sits somewhere between burgundy and oxblood — the colour of a serious Italian wine, appropriately enough.

The chairs are the real statement here. Their X-back frames play at architecture, with clean geometric lines that feel almost structural rather than decorative. The round tapered legs read like exclamation marks beneath each seat. Original black skai upholstery covers the seats — worn with the kind of honest patina that comes from fifty years of actual dinners, actual conversations, actual life. The lacquer finish shows the same characterful signs of use, adding depth rather than diminishing it.

This set is in good overall condition. The wear is genuine and consistent — not disguised but honoured. The table stands stable, the chairs are structurally sound, and the lacquer retains its warmth and lustre where it counts. Minor surface marks and gentle wear to the skai seats reflect five decades of daily use.

The design language here speaks to the Italian radical design movement of the 1970s, when furniture makers in Milan, Turin and beyond were experimenting with bold colour, geometric form and industrial materials. The architectural quality of the X-back chairs and the confident use of lacquer place this set firmly in that tradition, recalling the work of designers like Claudio Salocchi who brought sculptural ambition to domestic furniture.

As a complete dining set, these pieces create an instant atmosphere. They belong in a space that values personality over convention — a kitchen that doubles as a gallery, a dining room that hosts both Tuesday suppers and Saturday dinner parties with equal conviction.
