At over two metres tall, this Italian floor lamp does not illuminate a room so much as preside over it. Carved from deep, lustrous rosewood, its spindle-shaped body rises from the floor with the quiet authority of a chess piece — organic, tactile, and unmistakably handcrafted.

The rosewood itself is the material story here. Its grain swirls through the turned body with a richness that only this particular timber can deliver — deep burgundy, chocolate, and amber tones shifting as the light changes throughout the day. The geometric shade crowns the form with rhythmic patterning, filtering light into a warm, ambient glow that transforms the mood of any space.

This example is in good condition and fully functional. The rosewood body shows the gentle patina of five decades — a surface that has been polished by time rather than degraded by it. The shade is intact and contributes its own decorative presence to the overall composition.

The 1970s were a fertile period for Italian lighting design, sitting between the radical experimentation of the Castiglioni brothers and the coming Memphis explosion. Floor lamps like this one occupied a more artisanal corner of the market — pieces made by skilled turners working in noble materials, creating objects that were as much sculpture as lighting.

Positioned beside a reading chair, in a hallway, or as the anchor of a living room corner, this lamp brings vertical drama, warm light, and the unmistakable presence of Italian craft to its surroundings.
