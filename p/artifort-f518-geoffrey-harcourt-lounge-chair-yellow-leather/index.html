Yellow leather on a lip chair. It sounds bold, and it is. Geoffrey Harcourt's F518 for Artifort was already one of the most distinctive designs of the 1970s. Dressed in rich safran aniline leather, it becomes something truly unforgettable.

The aniline leather is the premium grade, dyed through rather than coated, which means the natural texture and grain of the hide remain visible and tactile. The safran yellow has a warmth and depth that coated leathers simply cannot achieve. Over time, aniline leather develops a patina that only enhances its character, aging with the kind of grace that synthetic materials never manage.

The F518's form needs little introduction. Designed by Harcourt in 1977, its lip-shaped silhouette is a playful commentary on the human face, curves that echo the mouth in a way that is simultaneously witty and deeply comfortable. The steel frame provides structural integrity beneath the sculpted foam, creating a chair that supports as well as it amuses.

Harcourt's relationship with Artifort spanned decades and produced a remarkable body of work. The F518, along with the F510 and F588, formed a trio of designs that established him as one of the most important furniture designers working in the Netherlands during the latter half of the twentieth century.

In an interior, this yellow F518 functions as more than furniture. It is a colour accent, a conversation piece, and a declaration of taste. It says something about the person who chose it: someone who understands that the best design is the kind that makes you smile.
